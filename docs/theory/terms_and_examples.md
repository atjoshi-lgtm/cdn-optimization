# Terms and examples

Here are the terms as they are used in this repository’s modeling context.

## Core terms

### Client metro / end-user metro / ASN metro
These are effectively the same role in this codebase: the metro where the demand originates.

In the served-from data, this is the field:

$$
\texttt{asn\_metro}
$$

It represents the metro associated with the users generating requests. In the optimization/performance model, this metro is the **source of traffic**.

So if traffic record $(u,m)$ exists, then:

- $u$ = client metro
- $u$ = end-user metro
- $u$ = ASN metro

and it means users in metro $u$ are being served by metro $m$.

### Edge metro / serving metro / bandwidth metro
These are also effectively the same role in this repo: the metro that serves the traffic for a client metro.

In the served-from data, this is the field:

$$
\texttt{bw\_metro}
$$

It is the metro from which the traffic is delivered to the users in the client metro.

So for traffic pair $(u,m)$:

- $u$ = client/end-user/ASN metro
- $m$ = edge/serving/bandwidth metro

---

## Traffic interpretation

If the data contains:

$$
Q_{u,m}
$$

that means:

> traffic originating from users in client metro $u$ is served by edge metro $m$.

This does **not** mean that $u$ and $m$ are neighbors in a purely geographic sense. It means the routing/serving relationship in the traffic data assigns some of $u$’s demand to $m$.

---

## Other related terms

### Metro
A node/location in the network model, usually represented by an airport code after conversion.

Examples in code are things like:

- `LAX`
- `ORD`
- `FRA`

A metro can appear in different roles depending on context:

- as a client metro,
- as a serving metro,
- as an MCH/parent metro.

### Neighborhood to
For a client metro $u$, `neighborhood_to[u]` is the list of serving metros used by that client metro.

Mathematically:

$$
\mathcal{B}_u = \{ m : Q_{u,m} > \theta \}
$$

So this is the set of metros that serve traffic for users in $u$.

### Neighborhood from
For a serving metro $m$, `neighborhood_from[m]` is the list of client metros whose users are served by $m$.

Mathematically:

$$
\mathcal{U}_m = \{ u : Q_{u,m} > \theta \}
$$

So this is the set of demand origins feeding into serving metro $m$.

### Incoming traffic at a serving metro
This is the total demand served by metro $m$:

$$
T_m^{\rm in} = \sum_{u} Q_{u,m}
$$

In code this is `INCOMING_TRAFFIC[m]`.

It is the traffic arriving into the serving role of metro $m$ from all client metros that use it.

### Traffic from a client metro
This is the total demand originating from client metro $u$:

$$
T_u^{\rm from} = \sum_m Q_{u,m}
$$

In code this is `TRAFFIC_FROM[u]`.

It is the total traffic generated by users in that metro.

---

## Parent / MCH terms

### Parent metro
A parent metro is the upstream metro used on cache misses.

If a request misses at serving metro $m$, the model assumes it goes to a parent MCH metro assigned to `m`.

### MCH metro
These are special metros that act as parent/upstream metros in the model.

A non-MCH metro is assigned its nearest MCH metro by distance.  
An MCH metro is its own parent.

So:

- serving metro handles the request first,
- if it misses, the request goes upstream to the parent/MCH metro.

### Midgress
Midgress is the traffic/latency on the path from serving metro to parent/MCH metro on a miss.

So if a request from users in $u$ is served by metro $m$ and misses there, the model adds:

- edge RTT from $u$ to $m$,
- then midgress RTT from $m$ to parent of $m$,
- then parent/MCH TAT.

---

## Practical picture

For one request, the modeled roles are:

1. **Users are in client/end-user/ASN metro** $u$
2. Request is initially handled by **edge/serving/bw metro** $m$
3. If cache hit at $m$, response is served there
4. If cache miss at $m$, request goes to **parent/MCH metro** $p(m)$

So the chain is:

$$
\text{client metro } u
\to
\text{serving metro } m
\to
\text{parent/MCH metro } p(m) \text{ on miss}
$$

## Important caveat

These terms are **repo-specific operational labels**, not universal networking definitions.

In particular:

- “ASN metro” here does not mean an autonomous system in the strict BGP sense.
- “edge metro” here means the serving metro in the model, not necessarily a specific hardware tier.
- “bw metro” is just the served-from dataset’s serving-location field.

#  One-request example

---

## One-request path example

Suppose:

- users are in **Seattle**
- Seattle traffic is served by **San Jose**
- San Jose’s assigned parent/MCH is **LAX**

Using symbols:

- client/end-user/ASN metro: $u = \text{SEA}$
- serving/edge/bw metro: $m = \text{SJC}$
- parent/MCH metro: $p(m) = \text{LAX}$

Suppose the served-from traffic matrix contains:

$$
Q_{\text{SEA},\text{SJC}} > 0
$$

This means some user traffic originating in Seattle is served by San Jose.

### Case 1: cache hit at the serving metro

A request from a Seattle user goes to San Jose.

Path:

$$
u \to m
$$

That is:

$$
\text{SEA} \to \text{SJC}
$$

The modeled latency components are:

1. edge RTT from Seattle to San Jose
2. edge TAT at San Jose

So the hit-path TTFB is roughly:

$$
R^{\rm edge}_{u,m} * A^{\rm edge}_m
$$

Interpretation:

- user is in the client metro Seattle,
- San Jose is the edge/serving metro,
- object is found in cache at San Jose,
- no parent lookup is needed.

### Case 2: cache miss at the serving metro

A request from a Seattle user again goes first to San Jose, but now the object is not in San Jose’s cache.

Path:

$$
u \to m \to p(m)
$$

That is:

$$
\text{SEA} \to \text{SJC} \to \text{LAX}
$$

The modeled latency components are:

1. edge RTT from Seattle to San Jose
2. little or no effective edge-hit TAT contribution on miss
3. midgress RTT from San Jose to LAX
4. parent/MCH TAT at LAX

So the miss-path TTFB is roughly:

$$
R^{\rm edge}_{u,m} * R^{\rm mch}_m * A^{\rm mch}_m
$$

Interpretation:

- Seattle is still the client/end-user/ASN metro,
- San Jose is still the serving/edge metro,
- LAX is the parent/MCH metro used because the serving metro missed.

### Where disk allocation matters

Suppose San Jose gets more disk:

$$
S_{\text{SJC}} \uparrow
$$

Then its hit rate rises:

$$
h_{\text{SJC}}(S_{\text{SJC}}) \uparrow
$$

So fewer Seattle requests follow the miss path through LAX.

That means:

- lower miss traffic cost at San Jose,
- better TTFB distribution for Seattle users whose traffic is served by San Jose.

### Client-level mixture example

Now suppose Seattle is served by two edge metros:

- San Jose with traffic $Q_{\text{SEA},\text{SJC}}$
- Los Angeles with traffic $Q_{\text{SEA},\text{LAX}}$

Then Seattle’s overall TTFB distribution is a weighted mixture:

$$
L_{\text{SEA}}(\mathbf{S})
=
\frac{
Q_{\text{SEA},\text{SJC}} L_{\text{SEA},\text{SJC}}(\mathbf{S})
+
Q_{\text{SEA},\text{LAX}} L_{\text{SEA},\text{LAX}}(\mathbf{S})
}{
Q_{\text{SEA},\text{SJC}} + Q_{\text{SEA},\text{LAX}}
}
$$

So a “client metro performance” is not just one path; it is the mixture of all serving paths used by that client metro.

# Symbol glossary for traffic-flow variables

Below is a glossary focused on the traffic-flow part of the formulation.

## Sets and indexing symbols

### $\mathcal{M}$
Set of all metros in the selected geography.

A metro may act as:

- a client metro,
- a serving metro,
- a parent/MCH metro.

### $\mathcal{A}$
Set of active metros included in evaluated optimization state.

These are metros with both:

- a footprint descriptor,
- a performance model.

### $u$
Client/end-user/ASN metro index.

This is the metro where the demand originates.

Repo meaning:
- “client metro”
- “end-user metro”
- “ASN metro”

all refer to this role.

### $m$
Serving/edge/bw metro index.

This is the metro that initially serves the request for client metro $u$.

Repo meaning:
- “serving metro”
- “edge metro”
- “bw metro”

all refer to this role.

### $p(m)$
Parent metro or MCH metro assigned to serving metro $m$.

If request misses at $m$, it is modeled as going to $p(m)$.

---

## Traffic matrix and neighborhood symbols

### $Q_{u,m}$
Traffic in Mbps from client metro $u$ to serving metro $m$.

Meaning:
- users located in metro $u$
- are served by metro $m$
- with aggregate demand $Q_{u,m}$

This is the most important traffic-flow quantity in the model.

Code mapping:
- `traffic_lookup_by_airport[(u,m)]`

### $\theta$
Traffic threshold used to decide whether a client-serving relationship is included in neighborhoods.

Only pairs with:

$$
Q_{u,m} > \theta
$$

are included in `neighborhood_to` / `neighborhood_from`.

Code mapping:
- `_TRAFFIC_THRESHOLD`

### $\mathcal{B}_u$
Set of serving metros used by client metro $u$.

Definition:

$$
\mathcal{B}_u = \{ m \in \mathcal{M} : Q_{u,m} > \theta \}
$$

Interpretation:
all edge/serving metros that carry nontrivial traffic for users in $u$.

Code mapping:
- `neighborhood_to[u]`

### $\mathcal{U}_m$
Set of client metros whose users are served by serving metro $m$.

Definition:

$$
\mathcal{U}_m = \{ u \in \mathcal{M} : Q_{u,m} > \theta \}
$$

Interpretation:
all demand-origin metros that feed traffic into serving metro $m$.

Code mapping:
- `neighborhood_from[m]`

---

## Aggregate traffic variables

### $T_m^{\rm in}$
Incoming traffic at serving metro $m$ in Mbps.

Definition:

$$
T_m^{\rm in}
=
\sum_{u \in \mathcal{U}_m} Q_{u,m}
$$

Interpretation:
total traffic served by edge metro $m$ from all client metros using it.

Code mapping:
- `INCOMING_TRAFFIC[m]`

Use in formulation:
- cost model

### $T_u^{\rm from}$
Traffic originating from client metro $u$ in Mbps.

Definition:

$$
T_u^{\rm from}
=
\sum_{m \in \mathcal{B}_u} Q_{u,m}
$$

Interpretation:
total user demand originating from metro $u$.

Code mapping:
- `TRAFFIC_FROM[u]`

Use in formulation:
- performance penalty scaling

### $G_u$
Traffic originating from client metro $u$ in Gbps.

Definition:

$$
G_u = \frac{T_u^{\rm from}}{1000}
$$

Interpretation:
same as $T_u^{\rm from}$ but converted from Mbps to Gbps for penalty weighting.

Use in formulation:
- `penalty_function(...)`

---

## Cache-hit / miss traffic variables

### $h_m(S_m)$
Cache hit-rate fraction at serving metro $m$ for provisioned disk $S_m$.

Definition:

$$
h_m(S_m) = \frac{H_m(S_m)}{100}
$$

where $H_m(S_m)$ is the FD hit rate percentage.

Interpretation:
fraction of requests at serving metro $m$ that are cache hits.

### $\mu_m(S_m)$
Cache miss fraction at serving metro $m$.

Definition:

$$
\mu_m(S_m) = 1 - h_m(S_m)
$$

Interpretation:
fraction of requests at serving metro $m$ that go upstream to parent/MCH.

### $M_m^{\rm rep}(S_m)$
Per-replica miss traffic at serving metro $m$.

Definition:

$$
M_m^{\rm rep}(S_m)
=
(1-h_m(S_m)) \frac{T_m^{\rm in}}{R_m}
$$

Interpretation:
miss traffic seen by one replica after traffic is evenly split across replicas.

Use in formulation:
- midgress cost
- parent-service cost

### $M_m(S_m)$
Aggregate miss traffic at serving metro $m$.

A convenient aggregate version is:

$$
M_m(S_m)
=
(1-h_m(S_m)) T_m^{\rm in}
$$

Interpretation:
total miss traffic generated by serving metro $m$ before replica splitting is algebraically canceled in total cost.

---

## Path and latency-flow symbols

### $R^{\rm edge}_{u,m}$
Edge RTT PDF from client metro $u$ to serving metro $m$.

Interpretation:
network round-trip latency between end users in $u$ and the serving edge metro $m$.

### $A_m^{\rm edge}$
Edge TAT PDF at serving metro $m$.

Interpretation:
service-time / processing-time distribution at the serving metro for edge handling.

### $R_m^{\rm mch}$
Midgress RTT PDF from serving metro $m$ to its parent/MCH metro.

Interpretation:
network latency incurred when a request misses at the edge and goes upstream.

### $A_m^{\rm mch}$
Parent/MCH TAT PDF at the parent of metro $m$.

Interpretation:
upstream service-time distribution at the parent tier.

### $L_{u,m}(\mathbf{S})$
TTFB PDF for client metro $u$ when traffic is served by metro $m$.

Interpretation:
full latency distribution for one client-serving path.

### $L_u(\mathbf{S})$
Client-level TTFB PDF for client metro $u$.

Definition:
traffic-weighted mixture of all serving-path PDFs for that client metro.

Interpretation:
overall latency distribution seen by users in metro $u$.
